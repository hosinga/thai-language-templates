Level 13 of 16 — beyond survival Thai

Understanding the Reply

You already know how to ask. This level is about what to do with what comes back — the part every phrasebook skips.

the single best fallback in any price negotiation เขียนให้ดูได้ไหม kǐan hâi duu dâi mǎi — "can you write it down?" numbers on paper beat numbers by ear, every time

Answering back: the echo system

Thai has no single word for "yes" or "no" — you answer by repeating the question's main verb, positive or negated.

ThaiSay itMeaning
⚠ there's no word for "yes"

Ask "mii wai-fai mǎi?" and the answer is just mii or mâi-mii — the verb from your own question, not a standalone yes/no word. Whatever verb you asked with, listen for that same verb coming back, plain or with mâi in front.

The number trap

At real speaking speed, the only difference between 4, 40, and 400 is the last syllable — that's where to put your attention.

ThaiSay itMeaning
⚠ the -sìp / -róoi trap

-sìp multiplies by ten, -róoi multiplies by a hundred, and both get said fast and dropped onto the end of a base number you already know. Train your ear on that last syllable specifically, not the whole word.

Confirm, don't guess

Echoing a number back is always faster and safer than pretending you caught it.

TemplateSay itMeaning

How to drill this

  1. Next time you ask any "___ mǎi?" question from an earlier level, say both possible echo answers out loud before you even hear the real one.
  2. When a price comes back fast, listen only for the last syllable — sìp or róoi — and let the base number fill itself in.
  3. The moment you're not sure you caught a number right, ask them to write it down. Every single time, no exceptions.

Sounding Less Like a Textbook

Same meaning, said the way a Thai friend actually says it. Nothing you've learned so far was wrong — it was formal, and formal isn't how peers talk to each other.

the word you learned as "we" เรา = also just "I" rao — used casually among friends and peers not a mistake if you hear it — that's just how friends talk

Textbook to casual

Everything here still means what you already learned — this is the version you'll actually hear among friends.

ThaiSay itMeaning
⚠ ná is the master casualizer

You already met นะ (ná) as a softener back in earlier levels — it's worth calling out on its own here. Tack it onto almost any sentence and it takes the edge off, turning a flat statement or request into something warmer and more casual, with no grammar change needed.

Where formal still matters

Casual register is for peers and friends — it's not a universal upgrade.

know your audience

Keep chǎn/pǒm instead of rao, and the fuller textbook phrasing generally, with monks, elders, officials, or anyone you'd address respectfully in English too. Casual register is for people your own age or closer, not a replacement for politeness — it's a second register to add, not a swap for the first.

How to drill this

  1. Recognition first: next time you hear "wàt-dii" or "bpai-nǎi-maa," notice you already know the formal version underneath it.
  2. Try swapping "chǎn/pǒm" for "rao" in one casual conversation with someone your own age — not with anyone you'd naturally be formal with in English either.
  3. Add "ná" to the end of one request today and notice how much softer it lands compared to leaving it off.
