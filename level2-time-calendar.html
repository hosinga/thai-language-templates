⚠ the six-hour clock

Thai traditionally splits the day into four 6-hour blocks with their own counting words and switch-over rules — a genuinely fiddly system that takes real study to construct correctly yourself. At this level, don't try to build exact clock times from scratch: ask gìi mohng and listen for the answer, or just say the hour number plus the part of the day below (e.g. "bàai sǎam mohng" — 3 in the afternoon) and you'll be understood.

Parts of the day & relative time

Stack one of these onto a day name or a time to anchor it — "prûng-níi cháo" = tomorrow morning.

How to drill this

  1. Say today's real day and date out loud every morning using the templates above — it's a genuine daily rep, not a made-up drill.
  2. Learn the seven day names as sounds first; the "wan" prefix means you're really memorising seven syllables, not fourteen.
  3. Don't fight the six-hour clock yet — lean on "gìi mohng" plus a part-of-day word until the full system is worth learning properly.
